== WHY THIS EXISTS ==

Prediction markets are at peak popularity.
Millions of users across a wide variety of markets:
  - Sports betting (largest)
  - Political events
  - World events
  - Just about anything else
Beyond that, the concept of prediction markets is familiar to an even larger audience due to mainstream exposure.

Short form video is the most consumed medium by watch hour.
Viral, attention grabbing content does not need to be hand made thanks to modern tooling.
Many popular and monetarily successful channels release generated content.
While there is a subset of users who will not watch AI generated content, the vast majority do not care as long as the video is interesting enough:
    - Covering topics the user cares about
    - The most obvious AI tells are masked
    - Content remains factual
    - Engaging presentation (snappy subtitles, interesting voiceover, eye catching visuals)

The marriage of the two _could_ result in content that hooks users who are aware of prediction markets.

Nobody is doing this well yet. Prediction market content on short form platforms is mostly
manual creators reacting to screenshots, or low-effort bot accounts. There is no automated,
quality-controlled pipeline turning market data into polished video.


== CORE INSIGHT: RETROSPECTIVE FIRST ==

The most reliably interesting content comes from markets that have already resolved.
You already know the ending — the job is finding the stories worth telling.

Why this beats a live-first approach:
  - Higher hit rate: you can cherry-pick outcomes that are genuinely surprising, dramatic,
    or absurd. No guessing whether something *might* become interesting.
  - No real-time infrastructure: no polling, no race against the clock, no stale content
    risk. The pipeline can run as a batch job on whatever schedule makes sense.
  - Evergreen backlog: resolved markets don't expire. A wild outcome from 6 months ago
    is just as watchable as one from yesterday if the viewer hasn't seen it before.
    "Did you know people bet on this?" works regardless of when it happened.
  - Content buffer: videos can be stockpiled and scheduled. Bad production day? Pull from
    the backlog. No pressure to ship something half-baked because the news cycle demands it.
  - Simpler V1: the entire real-time ingestion layer (polling, webhooks, staleness checks,
    race conditions) is deferred. V1 is just: pull resolved data, score it, generate video.

Live / trending market coverage is a natural expansion once the retrospective pipeline is
solid, but it is NOT required to launch or to produce consistently good content.


== WHAT SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE ==

V1 goal: a working pipeline that can produce 1-3 compilation-style short form videos per day
from resolved prediction market data, with minimal human effort (review + approve only).

Monetization path: platform ad revenue (YouTube Shorts, TikTok Creator Fund). This is a
content brand first, not a SaaS. Tooling can be productized later if the pipeline proves out.

Key metrics for V1:
  - Content quality: what % of generated videos pass human review without revision?
  - Publishing cadence: can we sustain 1-3 videos/day without running dry on material?
  - Engagement: views, watch-through rate, shares on published content.
  - Human time per video: target <5 minutes of review per published video.


== DATA SOURCES ==

Primary targets (all retain resolved market data accessible via API):
  - Polymarket — largest crypto prediction market, public API, wide topic coverage.
    Rich historical data with full odds history per market.
  - Kalshi — US-regulated, REST API, strong on politics/economics/events.
  - PredictIt — smaller but established, API available, political focus.

Secondary / enrichment:
  - Odds API (oddsapi.com) — aggregates sportsbook odds, useful for sports-adjacent markets.
  - News/context APIs — for adding background to resolved markets. When a market resolved
    because of a specific event, the video needs to explain what happened, not just show
    the odds chart. News API, Wikipedia recent events, etc.

Historical depth:
  - Start with the last 6-12 months of resolved markets across all platforms.
  - Older markets can be pulled in later, but will need more contextual enrichment
    since viewers may not remember the underlying event.
  - Build a local database of resolved markets so scoring and content selection can
    run without hitting external APIs repeatedly.

Constraints to track:
  - Rate limits per platform (Polymarket is generous, Kalshi is stricter)
  - ToS compliance — use official APIs, attribute properly
  - Pagination / bulk export — pulling historical data efficiently


== VIRALITY SCORING ==

The scoring model determines what content gets made. Since we're focused on resolved markets,
the signals are all backward-looking — we know exactly what happened and can score with
full information.

  1. Surprise factor — how unexpected was the outcome? A market that sat at 5% for weeks
     then resolved YES is inherently interesting. Score based on the gap between the
     prevailing odds and the actual resolution. Bigger upset = higher score.

  2. Narrative arc — did the odds tell a story? Markets that swung wildly before resolving
     are more compelling than ones that flatlined at 90% and resolved YES. The shape of
     the odds history curve matters: reversals, cliff drops, last-minute swings.
     The odds chart IS the visual content — a boring chart means a boring video.

  3. Absurdity / novelty — unusual or funny markets inherently attract clicks.
     "People actually bet $2M on whether a celebrity would dye their hair" is content
     regardless of the outcome. Could use an LLM classifier to rate "would this market
     title make someone stop scrolling?" on a 1-10 scale.

  4. Total volume — high-volume resolved markets had a lot of people with skin in the
     game. That makes the outcome more dramatic regardless of what it was.

  5. Historical significance — did the market track a major real-world event? Resolved
     markets tied to elections, disasters, scientific breakthroughs, etc. carry built-in
     narrative weight even months later.

  6. Topical relevance — is the resolved market's subject still in the public conversation?
     A 3-month-old market about a person who is trending today gets a boost.
     Cross-reference against current social/news signals.

V1 approach: start with a simple weighted formula across these signals. Tune weights manually
based on what actually performs well once content is live. ML-based scoring is a V2 concern.


== CONTENT FORMATS ==

Format 1: Retrospective Compilation (V1 target)
  "Markets you missed" / "You won't believe people bet on this" / "Wildest prediction
  market outcomes this month."
  Structure: 3-5 resolved market highlights per video, 10-20 seconds each, ~60-90s total.
  Each segment: what the market was, how the odds moved over time, what actually happened.
  The odds history chart is the visual centerpiece — it tells the story on its own.
  Can be batch-produced, scheduled, and stockpiled.

Format 2: Themed Compilations (V1 variant)
  Same structure but grouped by theme: "Wildest political bets," "Markets that flipped
  at the last second," "Biggest upsets," "Weirdest things people bet on."
  Themes help with discoverability and let the algorithm categorize the content.

Format 3: Standalone Story (stretch goal)
  A single resolved market that warrants deeper context. Think short-form video essay.
  "The time the internet bet $10M on whether [X] would happen — and lost."
  Requires: narrative scripting, more complex visual sequencing, possibly pulling in
  news clips or additional context. Significantly harder to automate.

Future: Live / Trending Content (expansion)
  Real-time coverage of markets as they move. Requires polling infrastructure, staleness
  management, faster pipeline turnaround. Layer this in once the retrospective pipeline
  is proven and the content brand has an audience.


== GENERATION PIPELINE ==

Stage 1: Data Ingestion & Scoring
  Input: resolved market data from platform APIs
  Process: pull resolved markets, compute virality score, rank and filter
  Output: ranked list of candidate markets for content
  Cadence: daily batch job (or on-demand)

Stage 2: Script Generation
  Input: top-scored markets + enrichment context (what actually happened, news context)
  Process: LLM generates a short narration script per market segment, plus an overall
  intro/outro for the compilation
  Output: timestamped script with segment boundaries
  Tools: OpenAI / Anthropic API

Stage 3: Voiceover
  Input: script text
  Process: TTS generation with a consistent "channel voice"
  Output: audio file per segment
  Tools: ElevenLabs (best quality/cost ratio for short form)

Stage 4: Visual Assembly
  Input: script + audio + market data (including odds history for chart animation)
  Process: programmatic video assembly — text overlays, animated odds charts, background
  footage/imagery, animated subtitles synced to voiceover
  Output: raw video file
  Tools: Remotion (React-based video) or FFmpeg pipeline with templated compositions.
  V1 should lean heavily on templated motion graphics over AI-generated imagery.
  Templates are faster, more consistent, and avoid the uncanny valley.

Stage 5: Human Review
  Input: rendered video + script + source data
  Process: reviewer checks factual accuracy, tone, visual quality
  Output: approved / rejected / sent back for revision
  Tools: simple web dashboard (could be as basic as a queue with approve/reject buttons)

Stage 6: Publishing
  Input: approved video + metadata (title, description, tags, thumbnail)
  Process: upload to target platforms via API
  Output: published video URLs
  Tools: YouTube Data API, TikTok Content Posting API, Instagram Graph API
  Note: each platform has its own upload specs, aspect ratios, and metadata requirements.


== HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP ==

Review checkpoints:
  1. Post-scoring: does the selected content actually make sense to cover?
     (lightweight — could be a daily digest email with approve/skip per item)
  2. Post-generation: is the finished video good enough to publish?
     (heavier — requires watching the video, checking facts)

Since retrospective content isn't time-sensitive, the reviewer doesn't need to be
on-call. Review can happen in a single daily batch — check the queue, approve/reject,
move on. This makes the human cost much more predictable and manageable.

Target: <5 minutes of human time per published video.


== LEGAL / COMPLIANCE ==

Things to get right early:
  - Data source ToS: use official APIs where available, attribute properly, don't
    redistribute raw data in ways that violate terms.
  - Platform content policies: TikTok/YouTube/Instagram all have rules about financial
    content. Prediction market coverage likely falls in a gray area — avoid anything
    that looks like financial advice or gambling promotion. Framing matters: "look at
    this interesting thing that happened" vs. "you should bet on X." Retrospective
    framing naturally helps here — we're reporting on what happened, not telling
    anyone to do anything.
  - Disclosure: if any content could be perceived as promotional, disclose. Better
    safe than demonetized.
  - Copyright: background footage/music must be licensed or royalty-free.


== TECH STACK (initial thoughts) ==

  - Language: Python (data pipeline, scoring, LLM integration) + TypeScript (review dashboard, Remotion video)
  - Queue/orchestration: simple job queue (Redis + BullMQ or similar), not a full workflow engine for V1.
    No real-time requirements since everything is batch-oriented.
  - Storage: S3-compatible object store for generated assets (audio, video, thumbnails)
  - Database: Postgres for resolved market data, scores, content status tracking
  - Deployment: containerized, could run on a single box for V1
  - CI: GitHub Actions for basic pipeline testing


== OPEN QUESTIONS ==

  - How far back should we go for historical markets? Last 6 months? Last year? All time?
    Older markets need more context for viewers who may not remember the event.
  - Should the channel have a "personality" / brand voice from day one, or iterate into one?
  - What's the minimum viable review dashboard, and can we skip it for V1 in favor of
    something simpler (e.g., Slack bot with approve/reject buttons)?
  - How many videos per day is sustainable before quality drops or platforms flag as spam?
  - What's the best way to source contextual info for resolved markets? Some outcomes are
    self-explanatory ("Did X win the election? Yes.") but others need a sentence or two
    of background. Can the LLM fill this in reliably, or do we need a news API?
  - When do we layer in live content? What signals tell us the retrospective pipeline is
    mature enough to expand?
